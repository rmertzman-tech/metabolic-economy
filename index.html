When Institutions Extract?

① Business as Usual — The Extraction Model

The institution pushes workers hard and creates bureaucratic friction that blocks their best efforts. Think of a nursing home chain or a private prison operator focused on quarterly returns. Workers accumulate psychological harm. The costs are invisible on the institution's balance sheet — but visible in the public health system, in emergency rooms, in workers' families.

How quickly do workers burn out?
This chart tracks worker count (left axis) and their ability to stay present and engaged at work (right axis) over time. Notice how fast both collapse.
17% of workers remain by the end. Present Integration (ability to focus and engage) falls from 0.85 to 0.09 — near zero.
What this means ethically

The extraction model does not randomly harm some workers. It systematically destroys the capacity for present engagement — the ability to actually do skilled, caring work — in everyone. A nursing home aide who has lost Present Integration cannot provide the attentive care that residents need, not because she stopped caring, but because the institutional structure depleted the neurological and psychological substrate of care.

Kant's humanity formula asks whether persons are being treated as ends in themselves. When an institution's design systematically degrades the capacities required for workers to be genuine agents — not just once but as a predictable structural outcome — the answer is clear.

Who pays for the damage?
The public deficit tracks what taxpayers spend repairing the harm the institution generates: healthcare costs, disability claims, social services, replacement workforce training. This is the externalized cost — real spending that never appears on the institution's profit-and-loss statement.
The institution generates harm it does not pay for. Taxpayers cover the difference.
The consequentialist view

Consequentialism asks: what are the actual downstream effects, and who bears the costs? In the extraction model, costs are systematically routed from the institution to the public. The institution's accounting looks efficient. The real accounting — including emergency room visits, mental health treatment, workforce dropout, family disruption — tells a different story. This is not a market failure. It is a market design: the incentive structure rewards externalizing harm onto those with the least power to resist it.

② The Welfare State Response — Treating the Symptom

We raise taxes on institutions and use the revenue to fund repair programs: healthcare subsidies, mental health services, workforce support. The institution continues operating exactly as before — same extraction pressure, same bureaucratic friction. But now there is a safety net. This is the political logic behind most regulatory responses to institutional harm.

Does the safety net protect workers?
Compare worker retention and engagement between Scenario 1 (Business as Usual) and Scenario 2 (Welfare State). The dotted line shows what the welfare state achieves.
Welfare state: 16% of workers remain — barely different from 17% without it. The safety net does not change the underlying mechanism.
The Tums Strategy

If you have chronic acid reflux, you can take antacids every day and manage the symptoms. You will feel slightly better. But if you do not change what you eat, the underlying condition continues and may worsen. The welfare state response to institutional harm works similarly. It funds repair without changing the mechanism generating the damage. Workers are slightly better supported as they burn out. They still burn out.

The simulation shows something worse: the welfare state actually generates a larger public deficit than the Business as Usual scenario because it taxes more but does not reduce the repair costs — the same harm keeps being generated at the same rate.

The Welfare State makes the deficit worse
Higher taxes collect more revenue — but the harm keeps being generated at the same rate, so repair costs remain equally high. The deficit diverges sharply from Scenario 1 before both stabilize when firms go bankrupt.
S2 deficit peaks at –474 (vs –149 for S1) before firms collapse. More spending, same damage.
What social contract theory says

Social contract theory — particularly Rawls's account of fair institutional arrangements — asks whether the people inside a system could reasonably endorse it from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which position they would occupy. No reasonable person, not knowing whether they would be a nursing home worker or a shareholder, would endorse an arrangement that systematically destroys workers' capacities while requiring taxpayers to subsidize the damage. The welfare state version of this arrangement is only marginally more defensible: it adds a more expensive subsidy while leaving the underlying arrangement intact.

③ Structural Change — The Biobond Model

Now something different happens. The institution's cost of borrowing money rises when its workers are suffering — this is the Biobond mechanism. Worker harm creates a financial liability that appears in the institution's cost of capital. Simultaneously, the institution reduces extraction pressure and bureaucratic friction. The financial structure itself creates an incentive to not harm workers.

What happens to workers under structural change?
Worker count and Present Integration (ability to engage and do skilled work) under the structural change model. This is the same 200 workers as Scenarios 1 and 2.
100% of workers remain throughout. Present Integration stays above 0.81 — nearly the starting level.
Why this is not utopian

The structural change scenario does not assume that institutions become virtuous. It assumes that the financial structure creates incentives that align institutional self-interest with worker wellbeing. The institution reduces extraction not because it cares about workers but because harming workers has become financially costly. This is the core argument of the Biobond financial instrument: make the externalized costs of workforce harm visible on the institution's balance sheet, and market mechanisms will do some of the work that regulation struggles to do.

Care ethics would note that this is still insufficient — treating workers as ends in themselves, not merely as cost factors, requires more than financial incentive alignment. But as a structural intervention, it represents a genuine change in the mechanism rather than a subsidy for the symptom.

What does the public pay in Scenario 3?
In the structural change model, the public initially pays for the transition — institutions face higher capital costs as they restructure. But repair costs fall dramatically because harm is being generated at a much lower rate.
S3 public deficit stabilizes at +155 — positive, meaning the system eventually runs a surplus as harm costs fall.
The ethical argument for structural change

The five ethical frameworks the course has covered converge on structural change more than on either the extraction model or the welfare state patch. Virtue ethics: workers in Scenario 3 can actually develop and exercise the capacities that make skilled care possible. Kantian ethics: the institutional design no longer systematically treats workers merely as extraction vehicles. Consequentialism: the aggregate harm is vastly lower, and the costs do not burden those with least power. Care ethics: the conditions for genuine responsiveness to the vulnerable — residents, inmates, clients — are preserved. Social contract: reasonable persons would endorse an arrangement where the institution bears the costs of the harm it generates.

This convergence across frameworks is what SAGE analysis is designed to identify. When multiple ethical traditions support the same practical direction, that convergence is itself significant evidence.

The structural ethics argument

The simulation does not tell you what to think. It shows you what the structural logic produces. The extraction model generates harm at a predictable rate. The welfare state patches the harm at a higher cost without reducing it. The structural change model aligns financial incentives with the conditions required for workers to actually do their jobs well.

None of this settles the ethical question — it illuminates it. Care ethics would note that even Scenario 3 treats workers primarily as cost factors rather than as persons with irreducible dignity and relational obligations. The SAGE analysis the course asks you to do would apply all five frameworks to a specific institutional situation and ask: where do they converge, where do they diverge, and what does the pattern of convergence suggest about what structural change is ethically required?
